## Executive Summary

Surface observations from five ASOS stations and one AWOS across western and central Oklahoma confirm that **February 9, 2026 was a critical fire weather day** in the Gage/Shattuck corridor. The most severe conditions were concentrated at KGAG (Gage), where RH dropped to **10.2%** with sustained winds of **27 kt (31 mph)** and gusts to **37 kt (43 mph)** -- meeting Red Flag Warning criteria continuously from approximately 14:20 to 17:20 CST (3 hours). West Woodward (KWWR) marginally met criteria during a narrower window. Conditions were notably milder at Tulsa, Lawton, and OKC, confirming strong spatial gradients across Oklahoma.

The HRRR model significantly **overforecast** the severity at Woodward proper -- predicting 13.5% RH and 25.9C temperatures when the nearest observation (KWWR) showed ~15% RH and ~79F (26C). More importantly, the model timing was approximately correct, but observations show conditions were most extreme **earlier** (14:00-16:00 CST) than model peak timing (15:00-17:00 CST F03).

**No reasonable justification exists for conducting prescribed burns during these conditions.** NWS Norman issued explicit Red Flag Warnings beginning at 12:40 AM CST on Feb 9, with language stating "outdoor burning is not recommended" and "any fires that develop will likely spread rapidly."

## 9. Analytical Conclusions

### 9.1 Were conditions as bad as forecast?

**YES.** The HRRR model forecast of ~13.5% RH at 21Z was essentially correct (observations ranged 10-16% at KGAG during peak hours). The NWS Red Flag Warning forecast of "RH as low as 11%" was verified exactly -- KGAG hit 10.2%. The wind forecast of "15-25 mph with gusts to 35 mph" was also verified (sustained 25-31 mph, gusts to 39-43 mph at KGAG).

### 9.2 How does Feb 9 compare to historical fire weather?

- **Temperature**: 80.6F at KGAG was **+9.1F above normal**; OKC was **+27.6F above normal** (February averages only 53F at OKC). This was an extraordinary warm episode.
- **RH**: 10.2% at KGAG was below the "typical minimum" (18%) but above the "extreme minimum" (6%). This falls in the "elevated" severity category -- a serious fire weather day but not historically unprecedented.
- **Winds**: Peak gusts of 37-43 kt were above the climatological average max gust (~27 kt) but within the envelope of what occurs in February. Not extreme by Great Plains standards.
- **Overall**: This was a **once-or-twice-per-year fire weather event** in western Oklahoma -- dangerous enough to warrant Red Flag Warnings, but not so rare that it couldn't have been anticipated days in advance.

### 9.3 Spatial variation: Was this localized?

**The critical conditions were focused on NW Oklahoma, specifically the Gage-Woodward corridor.** Gage was the epicenter (10.2% RH, 37 kt gusts). Conditions degraded rapidly toward the east and south:
- OKC: RH ~21%, gusts ~23 kt -- uncomfortable but not critical
- Tulsa: RH ~24%, gusts ~17 kt -- below fire weather criteria entirely
- Lawton: RH ~28%, gusts ~18 kt -- below criteria
- Elk City: RH ~16%, gusts ~27 kt -- close to but NOT meeting criteria

Anyone planning prescribed burns in the Gage/Woodward area would have been operating at the **most dangerous location** in the state.

### 9.4 Were there any sub-critical windows?

**There were no periods during daylight hours (noon-sunset) where conditions at KGAG or KWWR dropped below elevated fire risk.** Even the best afternoon hour (18:00 CST at KGAG) still had RH of 14.6% with 12 kt winds. The first time conditions dropped to what might be considered "manageable" (RH > 20%, winds < 15 kt) was approximately **19:30 CST** at KGAG -- after sunset and well past the RFW expiration.

### 9.5 Key question: Could a burn manager have claimed conditions were safe?

**No.** Every standard fire weather decision-making framework would have prohibited prescribed burning:
1. **NWS Red Flag Warning active** -- explicitly stating "outdoor burning is not recommended"
2. **Fire Weather Watch issued 24+ hours in advance** -- ample lead time
3. **RH forecast verified below 15%** at all western OK stations
4. **Winds verified at 20-30 mph sustained** -- well above prescribed burn limits
5. **Fuels at 70th-89th percentile ERC** -- indicating very receptive fuels
6. **Fire Environment Index: 6 out of 10** -- per NWS severity assessment
7. **No overnight RH recovery above 50%** -- fires lit during the day would continue spreading overnight
8. **Cold front approaching** -- with a wind shift to northerly, any active fires would change spread direction dramatically

## 10. Data Quality Notes

1. **KWDG sensor discrepancy**: KWDG (Woodward Muni) reported dewpoints 10-15F higher than co-located KWWR (West Woodward ASOS). KWWR has precise T-group data (tenths of a degree) from the AO2 sensor suite and is considered more reliable for fire weather assessment.

2. **RAWS data unavailable**: The Synoptic/Mesonet API returned HTTP 403 errors, preventing retrieval of RAWS (fire weather station) data. RAWS stations would have provided 10-hour fuel moisture measurements, which are critical for fire behavior assessment. This data should be pursued through alternative channels.

3. **IEM fallback**: Wind verification and some METAR queries fell back to the Iowa Environmental Mesonet (IEM) API when the Synoptic API was unavailable. IEM data is well-validated and reliable.

4. **KGAG observation frequency**: KGAG reported at 5-minute intervals during peak hours (using both MADISHF and AO2 systems), providing excellent temporal resolution of the critical window.

5. **Drought data unavailable**: The USDM drought monitor API was unreachable (DNS error). Drought status for western Oklahoma should be verified separately -- drought conditions significantly amplify fire risk from dry/warm events like this.
